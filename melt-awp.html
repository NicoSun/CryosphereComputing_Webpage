Ice Melting Arctic Albedo-Warming Potential

Beta phase product. May change slightly

Year: 2000

The Ice-Melt AWP is the AWP model applied only over a sea ice concentration of 25% or more. Ice-free regions do not accumulate anything as there is no ice to melt. Instead of an area unit (MJ per m2) ice-melt AWP is measured in absolute energy values (TJ). This change better reflects the larger melting potential of large regions.
Counting AWP only over sea ice results in low values appearing both in warm and cold regions. In a warm region there is no ice to melt at all. In cold regions with high ice concentration and a high albedo the ice doesnâ€™t absorb much sunlight and melting from sunshine remains low throughout the summer.

The conditions in regions with high values are dispersed sea ice, melt ponds and the ice edge. Due to the sensor limitations and large grid cell size the sea ice edge does show exaggerated values. Grid cells near the coast experience a coastline measurement error. To reduce this noise the lower limit of sea ice concentration was chosen to be 25% and not 15% as used in sea ice extent measurements.

The long term downwards trend is caused by reduced sea ice cover and a general northward movement of the melting regions. The amount of solar energy received is the same every year. Greenhouse gases (not accounted for in this model) are not an extra energy input into the system. They only insulate it (in terms of radiation) against the coldness of space.

Even though this is a very limited model for ice melting physics large, high AWP Areas correlate well with real the world main melting areas. (Northern Greenland Sea, Eastern-Central Beaufort Sea) Likewise, areas with low AWP for example southern Beaufort and Laptev Sea never experience high melt because the sea ice just drifts away.

Despite the simple physics and less meaningful values the spatial distribution seen in maps and decadal changes seen in graphs can give a better understanding of the melting processes.
